# Coptic / Co-optic

## The Validation Cascade, Chaos Arbitrage, and the Mechanics of Cultural Erasure by Absorption

The wordplay is structural. *Coptic* and *co-optic* share their first syllable and their sound, and the second term describes exactly what happened to the first. The Coptic church, the Christian community of Egypt and Ethiopia, whose tradition stretches back to the first century CE, whose theology was articulated by Origen and Clement of Alexandria, whose monastic practices gave the entire subsequent development of Christian asceticism its founding grammar: by the time Anthony the Great died in 356, Athanasius of Alexandria (the Coptic patriarch who had articulated the Nicene formulation) recorded that "the desert had become a city." The Desert Fathers' practices reached the Western church through John Cassian, whose writings Benedict of Nursia placed at the foundation of his Rule; they reached the Eastern church through the traditions that became Athos. The iconographic tradition of the Coptic church is among the oldest continuous visual vocabularies in the world. This community was *co-opted*: its substance was absorbed by the tradition it had helped generate, while its specific cultural identity was progressively rendered invisible.

To be co-opted is not to be destroyed. It is, in many respects, worse. Destruction preserves the distinction between the thing and the thing that destroyed it. Absorption erases the distinction: it folds the absorbed culture into the absorbing one so thoroughly that the absorbed culture's contributions become invisible as contributions and appear instead as natural properties of the absorbing tradition. The source community is not erased. It is made invisible while present: its symbols, its forms, its theological vocabulary are everywhere in the dominant tradition, attributed to the dominant tradition, while the community that originated them is peripheral, marginalised, and frequently unknown to the people who daily use what that community produced.

## I. The Validation Cascade

The *Validation Cascade* is the mechanism by which this process becomes self-reinforcing and irreversible.

A Validation Cascade is any system in which each enforcement or adoption event legitimises the preceding one, making the originating error permanently invisible. The structure is simple: an initial act of appropriation or misattribution is followed by a second act that treats the first as established fact; the second act is followed by a third that treats both preceding acts as established fact; and so on, until the chain of legitimising events has accumulated sufficient institutional weight that questioning the originating act requires dismantling the entire chain.

The religious example: the Nicene Creed (325 CE) drew on the theological vocabulary that had been developed in Alexandria over the preceding two centuries (by Clement, by Origen, by Athanasius, who was himself Coptic) and encoded it as the orthodox standard of the Roman imperial church. The ecumenical council's authority legitimised the specific theological vocabulary choices. The subsequent history of the Roman church treated those choices as originating with the council's authority rather than with the Alexandrian tradition the council had drawn from. Each subsequent theological development that built on the Nicene formulation legitimised the Nicene formulation's claim to origination. The Coptic community's contribution to the vocabulary in which the formulation was expressed became invisible as a contribution: it was now simply the vocabulary of Christianity, not the vocabulary of a specific community that the Roman church had absorbed and not fully credited.

This is the Cascade structure: each adoption event legitimises the preceding one, the chain accumulates weight, and the originating act (the initial absorption without full attribution) recedes behind the density of subsequent legitimisation.

The Cascade operates across every domain where one cultural or intellectual tradition absorbs material from another. It is the mechanism by which blues became rock and roll without transferring the economic rights. It is the mechanism by which the mathematical traditions of the Islamic Golden Age became "European mathematics" in the narrative of the Scientific Revolution. It is the mechanism by which yoga became a wellness industry product without the corresponding recognition of the philosophical tradition that produced it. The originating community is not absent from the dominant culture. It is present in the dominant culture's DNA, unacknowledged.

## II. Chaos Arbitrage

The *Chaos Arbitrage* is the Validation Cascade's active operating partner: the mechanism by which a dissenting movement's energy is captured and redirected to serve the status quo.

Punk is the primary English-language example, and the mechanism begins, as it always does, with the word.

*Punk* arrives in English with a resume of contempt. Variously: a catamite, a young man used sexually; a prostitute; a petty criminal; a worthless person; a coward. In prison slang, the weakest figure in the hierarchy, the one who submits, who cannot defend himself. The word carried the full register of social abjection. There is an older British meaning (touchwood, tinder, material that catches fire easily) but it had largely been displaced by the contempt register long before it was handed to the subculture.

The word was not chosen by the communities it named. The label *punk rock* had been coined in 1971 by Dave Marsh, an American music journalist writing in *Creem*, applied initially to US garage bands. By the time the British scene crystallised around 1976-78, the label was already five years old and American in origin, attached by the same press apparatus that would subsequently co-opt what it was naming. The communities absorbed the label and wore it. *I am what you called me. It is not what you meant.*

Punk was, in its specific cultural moment (1976-1978), a genuinely dissenting practice: a formal rejection of the technical virtuosity and commercial production values of mainstream rock, a community-organised performance and distribution network that operated outside the major label infrastructure, a visual vocabulary of deliberate ugliness and refusal that was designed to be unassimilable by the commercial apparatus. The Sex Pistols were dropped from A&M Records in 1977 after a week, their contract bought out because the label could not manage what it had signed.

That visual vocabulary was itself a second act of reclamation. The punk aesthetic co-opted the signifiers of poverty and abjection: torn clothing, safety pins repurposed as fasteners, bin liners worn as garments, haircuts administered without professional equipment, and converted them into deliberate statement. The visual vocabulary of the person who cannot afford better, redeployed as the visual vocabulary of the person who refuses what better represents. Poverty not as condition to be escaped but as politics to be worn. The signifiers of abjection absorbed and re-encoded as a grammar of refusal: the same move the word itself had already performed.

Malcolm McLaren (manager of the Sex Pistols, co-designer with Vivienne Westwood of the visual vocabulary) was always partly front-running the Chaos Arbitrage. McLaren had managed the New York Dolls. He had watched the commercial machine fail to absorb them and noted the dynamics. He was explicit about the operating principle, not in analysis but in self-description: *"Plagiarism is what the word is about. If you didn't start seeing things and stealing because you were so inspired by them, you'd be stupid."* He was describing his art practice. The description applies to the broader operation. The Sex Pistols project was, from its architecture, a knowing engagement with the co-option mechanism: a calculation that if the Arbitrage was inevitable, maximum value should be extracted before the wave broke. *Never Mind the Bollocks* is simultaneously a commercial artefact and an artefact of anti-commercialism. That tension was not a contradiction McLaren failed to resolve. It was the product.

The word *punk* contains, in its older meaning, its own epitaph for the project: touchwood. Material that catches fire easily. It did.

Within three years, punk was a fashion category at mainstream retailers. Within five, the major labels had developed the mechanisms for signing, producing, and marketing punk-adjacent acts with sufficient production values to be commercially viable while retaining enough of the surface vocabulary to be legible as authentic. The energy of the dissent (the genuine anger, the formal refusal, the community organisation) had been captured, stripped of its political content, and repackaged as aesthetic product.

The *Chaos Arbitrage* is the professional competence of the dominant system's recovery operation: the ability to take the energy generated by a challenge to the status quo and convert it into value for the status quo, at speed and at scale, before the challenging movement can entrench itself sufficiently to resist.

Black Lives Matter, in its 2020 iteration, generated the most rapid and comprehensive corporate Chaos Arbitrage in recent cultural history. Within weeks of the George Floyd protests, major corporations were publishing statements of support, updating their brand guidelines, making donations to Black-led organisations, and commissioning advertising campaigns featuring Black talent. The diversity and inclusion industry, already well-established, received a significant capital injection. By 2023, several of the most prominent corporate diversity initiatives had been quietly discontinued or scaled back, while the corporations that had launched them continued to trade on the cultural credibility the statements had generated.

The Chaos Arbitrage does not require malicious intent. It requires only that the dominant system's recovery mechanisms (the PR apparatus, the brand management infrastructure, the corporate diversity and inclusion framework) be faster and better-resourced than the challenging movement's ability to enforce accountability for the commitments generated. The challenge generates the energy; the Arbitrage captures it; the status quo is reinforced, with enhanced credibility from having been seen to engage with the challenge.

## III. The Cheetopolver Risk

The *Cheetopolver Risk* is the Chaos Arbitrage as applied to concepts, names, and intellectual frameworks: the co-option of an originating term or idea by a larger commercial or institutional entity, which then deploys the term or idea stripped of its origin, erasing the originating community's claim to it in the process.

The term *Cheetopolver* was coined in a specific intellectual context (the cultural analysis of commercial brand language and the organic naming practices that corporate branding displaces) before it was encountered by the commercial context it describes. Its value as a theoretical term derives precisely from that origin: it was named by someone outside the commercial apparatus, using the kind of lateral, pattern-attentive intelligence that the manufactured deficit of the everyman systematically devalues, and the name carries the epistemological authority of the person who was paying attention.

The Cheetopolver Risk is the possibility that a concept generated outside the commercial apparatus is absorbed into the commercial apparatus, stripped of its origin, redeployed in a commercial register, and in the process made to serve exactly the thing it was originally describing. The Chaos Arbitrage applied to intellectual property: the dissenting concept as raw material for the dominant system's self-legitimisation.

The Cheetopolver Risk gives the concept its theoretical weight by carrying its own etymology. It originated in cultural domain analysis. It was then found applicable to brand parasitism and media co-option. The origin gives the concept authority precisely because it demonstrates the mechanism it describes: a term coined to describe commercial erasure of organic naming was itself subject to commercial erasure risk. The analysis and the example are the same object. The full case study (the Frito-Lay campaign, the SEO burial of the correct term, and the mechanics of corporate linguistic displacement) is documented in *The Year They Bought the Dictionary*.

## IV. Co-optic: The Dominant Culture and the Minority Symbol

The Coptic case is exemplary because the mechanism is visible across multiple registers simultaneously.

In iconography: the *Chi-Rho* symbol (the overlapping Greek letters Chi and Rho, the first two letters of *Christos*) appears in the Coptic tradition from the third century CE, in manuscript illumination, in textile work, in funerary art. It was adopted by Constantine as the imperial symbol of Christianity following the Battle of Milvian Bridge (312 CE). In the subsequent history of Western Christianity, the Chi-Rho is a Roman imperial symbol with specifically Western associations. The Coptic origin is not erased (scholars know it) but it is invisible in the dominant narrative.

In theology: the *homoousios* formulation (*of the same substance*, the term encoded in the Nicene Creed to settle the Arian controversy) was the formulation associated with Athanasius of Alexandria, the Coptic theologian who spent most of his episcopal career in exile for defending it against the Arian position that had Roman imperial support. The Nicene orthodoxy is named for the council, not for Athanasius. The formulation is presented as the Roman imperial church's theological settlement. The Coptic theologian who defended it at personal cost across decades is a footnote.

In monasticism: the Egyptian desert fathers (Anthony the Great, Pachomius, Macarius) developed the practices of Christian monasticism in the fourth century CE. Every subsequent monastic tradition in Western Christianity (the Benedictines, the Cistercians, the Dominicans, the Franciscans) traces its structural origins to practices first developed in Coptic Egypt. The Coptic origin is acknowledged in specialist historical literature and invisible in popular accounts of Christian monasticism, which typically begin with Benedict of Nursia in the sixth century.

The pattern in each case is identical: the Coptic community's contribution is absorbed by the dominant tradition, the absorption is legitimised by each subsequent use of the absorbed material, and the Coptic origin becomes a specialist footnote rather than a central attribution. The Validation Cascade makes the originating community's claim progressively harder to articulate: each generation of scholarship that treats the Nicene formulation as originating with the council rather than with Alexandrian theology adds another layer of legitimisation to the misattribution.

## V. The Street Names Itself

There is a form of community that names itself under conditions of surveillance. The encoding is the first act of resistance: to develop a vocabulary legible only to those inside the community is to construct a perimeter that official language cannot cross without declaring itself an intruder.

In the inner-west and inner-south suburbs of Sydney from the late 1980s into the early 1990s (Tempe, Sydenham, St Peters, the corridors of public housing along the Illawarra Line), a subculture developed whose linguistic signature was a hybrid system: pig Latin inversion combined with verlan-style syllabic reversal. The dominant account of the term *eshay* traces it to pig Latin for *sesh* or *seshie* (Australian slang for a drug-use session), with the initial consonant moved to the end and the suffix added: *esh-say*, compressed under the speed of daily speech to *eshay*. A secondary derivation traces it through pig Latin for *yes*: *yesway*, clipped to *esway*, further reduced to *eshay*. The seshie derivation is phonologically the more economical and situates the term in the specific context of the community that coined it. Either way the mechanism is the same: a term of mutual recognition between members that became, in the mouths of those outside the community, a term of contempt. The system was productive: *adlay* (pig Latin for *lad*) ran alongside it, extending the argot. The broader lexicon combined pig Latin base encoding with syllabic reversal as a further layer of opacity. The community had named itself in a language that required belonging to parse. The dominant culture took the name and returned it as stigma.

This is the mirror image of the Coptic mechanism, and in some respects the more aggressive form. Where the Coptic community's contribution was absorbed *upward*, its theological vocabulary and iconographic forms taken into the dominant tradition and stripped of attribution while the source community was rendered peripheral, the eshay community's self-description was absorbed *as weapon*. The label was not appropriated for its value. It was inverted and returned as a marker of disgust. The community was not made invisible. It was rendered hypervisible in the worst possible register: legible as a threat, illegible as a culture.

In the banlieues north and east of Paris across the same decade, a structurally cognate encoding practice developed among the children of North African and West African immigrants. The convergence was not coincidental. Communities under comparable conditions of surveillance and social abjection arrive independently at the same solution: syllabic encoding as perimeter, the perimeter as community. *Verlan* (derived from *l'envers*, "backwards") was a systematic syllabic inversion of standard French that created a private register: *femme* became *meuf*, *l'envers* itself became *verlan*, *Français* became *céfran*. The encoding was the community; the community was the encoding. To speak verlan was to signal membership. To understand verlan was to have been there.

On 20 June 2005, Nicolas Sarkozy (Interior Minister of France, not yet President) visited La Courneuve, a housing project north of Paris, in the aftermath of a child's death from a stray bullet. Sidi-Ahmed Hammache, eleven years old, had been killed the previous day by two stray bullets outside his home in the Cité des 4000. Sarkozy went to the family's apartment to offer condolences.

What he said there was not said for cameras. The journalist Marion Van Renterghem, of *Le Monde*, had been in the apartment for hours before Sarkozy arrived, speaking with the family; she had not been identified as press, and Sarkozy's team did not ask. Inside the apartment, before the grieving family of a dead eleven-year-old, Sarkozy made three announcements. The second was this:

*Dès demain, on va nettoyer au Kärcher la cité des 4000.*

From tomorrow, we are going to pressure-wash the Cité des 4000 with a Kärcher. Later the same day, addressing residents and cameras in public, he repeated the intention to clean the estate, but this time dropped the word *Kärcher*. The machine was for the family. The sanitised version was for broadcast.

On 29 June, returning to La Courneuve, Sarkozy was challenged on the phrase. He did not retract it. He defended it with a word that repays attention.

*Le terme "nettoyer au Kärcher" est un terme qui s'impose.*

*S'impose*: imposes itself. Is self-evidently appropriate. Is the only word that fits. He was not conceding rhetorical excess. He was asserting that industrial-cleaning language for human beings in public housing was the precise, inevitable vocabulary. The term does not reach for effect. It settles.

In October 2005, at Argenteuil (a separate visit, a different housing estate, a different suburb), Sarkozy addressed residents from a tower block and used a different word. *Racaille*: riffraff, scum, the sediment at the bottom of the social order. *Vous en avez assez de cette bande de racailles? Et bien on va vous en débarrasser.* Are you sick of this gang of scum? We're going to get rid of them for you.

By 2016, standing for the Republican primary, Sarkozy was still defending both. Asked about his language on France 2, he said: *Quand je dis "on va nettoyer au Kärcher", c'est pas une transgression, c'est une réalité, et j'aurais dû le faire plus.* It's not a transgression. It's a reality. And I should have done it more.

*Kärcher* is a genericised trademark. In French (as *hoover* in English), the brand name has absorbed the category. To *passer au Kärcher* is to pressure-wash, regardless of which manufacturer's equipment is deployed, in the same way that to *hoover* is to vacuum irrespective of whether the machine is a Dyson or a Henry. The genericised trademark has become the verb for the action. Hoovering sucks: this mild pun marks the outermost edge of what a genericised vacuum cleaner brand is ordinarily asked to carry.

Kärcher was asked to carry more. The company (a 70-year-old family-owned German manufacturer whose products had cleaned Mount Rushmore) sent letters to all twelve presidential candidates in the 2007 French election and took out newspaper advertisements, formally distancing itself from the use of its name. Its spokesman said the company "cannot recognize itself in the recent words and confusion to which its name has been associated." It was, Patrice Anderouard said, a matter of values: a family company, well-entrenched values, that didn't match these comments. The action Sarkozy proposed to take with their product was not the cleaning of a surface. Sarkozy's office had no comment.

By the time the letters went out, the verb had already escaped its author. Jean-Marie Le Pen, campaigning in Argenteuil in 2007, used it against Sarkozy's policy while speaking sympathetically to residents: *"If some want to Kärcher-ize you, to exclude you, we want to help you get out of these ghettos."* The far right was now deploying the word to condemn the centre-right's immigration rhetoric, the Chaos Arbitrage completing its circuit. A brand name coined as a cleaning metaphor, applied to a human population, had genericised into a cross-partisan political verb for ethnic purging. The company that cleaned monuments was now the company whose name meant something else entirely. Kärcher's sales in France rose in 2006. Their spokesman noted that customers could "put things into perspective." The brand survived. The metaphor did not go away.

It didn't go away because it didn't need to. In January 2022, Valérie Pécresse, running in the Republican primary for the French presidency, announced: *Je vais ressortir le Kärcher de la cave. Ça fait dix ans qu'il y est et il est temps de l'utiliser.* I'm going to get the Kärcher out of the cellar. It's been there ten years and it's time to use it. The metaphor had survived long enough to be retrieved. Sarkozy's word had become an heirloom of the French right: a transferable political asset, stored, dusted off, deployed by successors who needed to signal toughness without the inconvenience of having originated the thought. Researchers reviewing Sarkozy's actual record were less sentimental. *En définitive*, concluded Mathieu Zagrodski of the Centre de recherches sociologiques sur le droit et les institutions pénales, *sa déclaration sur le Kärcher, tout comme celle sur les racailles, n'a rien donné de concret.* His declaration about the Kärcher, like that about the racaille, produced nothing concrete. The Kärcher was language. The language was the entire event.

The register gap between the two genericised trademarks is the argument in miniature. Same linguistic mechanism (proprietary eponym absorbed as common verb), entirely different social freight. And *Kärcher* is German. Winnenden, Baden-Württemberg. Whether the choice of German industrial engineering as the metaphor for what to do with North African residents of French public housing was conscious or not, it does not land neutrally.

The banlieue community's counter-move was verlan. *Racaille* (Sarkozy's word at Argenteuil, the word he said he should have deployed more) became *kaïra*. The syllables inverted, the contempt absorbed, the label returned as badge. *Je suis kaïra.* I am what you called me. It is not what you meant.

This is the counter-co-optic: not a refusal of the dominant culture's contempt but an absorption of it: the Chaos Arbitrage performed from the inside by the community the Arbitrage was designed to contain. The contempt is taken in, encoded, and reissued carrying different information. The Validation Cascade cannot build on this material, because the material has changed ownership at the point of contact.

In Sydney, the mechanism resolved further. The eshay community's response to stigmatisation generated fiction: *Housos*, a television comedy set in Australian public housing, in which a character called Jimmy the Junky embodied the subculture with sufficient fidelity that he developed enough cultural traction to escape the show's frame. Jimmy acquired a spinoff. He existed in the YouTube ecosystem without the show's architecture to hold him. The stigmatised identity had accumulated sufficient cultural weight to sustain independent content: the fictional character real enough to inhabit the same territory as the community he had been drawn from.

Then the final move: Spanian. A real person. A YouTube channel. The eshay aesthetic deployed deliberately for audience engagement (the language, the gesture, the visual grammar of the subculture), offered as content to an audience that includes precisely those who had applied the stigma. By his own public account, Spanian was not simply a member of the subculture he now documents: before the channel, he was a distributor of heroin and cocaine, operating in the same communities the eshay subculture inhabited. A distribution network that at its peak was turning over a hundred thousand dollars a week. He was not just an eshay. He was supplying the eshays. The lived experience is not incidental to the content. It is the content's entire claim to authority.

The arc is almost too clean to be accidental: from supplying the subculture's criminal economy to supplying the mainstream's content economy, with the subculture itself as the product in both transactions. Different supply chain. Same supplier. The credibility that the dominant culture's entertainment apparatus cannot manufacture (because it cannot be performed into existence, only accumulated through presence) is exactly what Spanian's channel monetises. Spanian does not merely reclaim the badge, as *kaïra* reclaims *racaille*. Spanian sells the badge back to the people who affixed it, with the full weight of a biography that proves it was never theirs to affix. The contempt has been so thoroughly owned that it generates subscription revenue from the contemptuous audience.

The loop, compressed:

*Organic self-naming. External contempt label. Internal reclamation of the label. Fictionalisation. The fictional character escaping the fiction. The real community monetising the stigma. The mainstream paying for what it once policed.*

The Housos detail is important: the show did not merely represent the subculture. It prepared an audience to receive Spanian. The dominant culture's entertainment apparatus created the market that the subculture would subsequently sell to. The fiction arrived first and made the authentic legible at scale: the co-option mechanism running in reverse, the mainstream's appetite for the transgressive converted into a distribution channel for the transgressive community's own product.

The Validation Cascade, at this point, has been interrupted. Not by demolition. By ownership. Not by dismantling the chain of legitimisation event by event. By refusing to let the chain begin.

## VI. Name the Cascade, Name the Originator, Reclaim the Cheetopolver

The correction to the Validation Cascade is not the demolition of the tradition that absorbed the originating community's contribution. It is the identification of the origination point (the specific cultural, intellectual, or community source from which the absorbed material was taken) and the insertion of that identification into every context where the material appears without attribution.

This is the Archivist of the Real function applied to cultural history: holding the correct attribution against the search landscape's amnesia. The person who says *this is Coptic* when the dominant tradition says *this is Roman* is performing the same act as the person who says *Cheetopolver* when the search landscape says *Cheetle*. The specific, experientially accurate attribution against the most frequently reinforced baseline.

The regulatory application is structurally identical. An industry body that funds itself through fees from the companies it regulates is a Validation Cascade in institutional form: each enforcement action it takes legitimises its authority as a regulator, while the underlying conflict of interest (the funding relationship that makes genuine enforcement structurally improbable) recedes behind the accumulated weight of enforcement events. The body appears to function because it produces decisions. The decisions appear legitimate because a recognised body produced them. The question of whether a body funded by the industry it polices can ever find decisively against that industry is not raised by the Cascade; it is buried by it. Naming the mechanism is the first step toward asking the question the mechanism was designed to prevent.

Every Validation Cascade has an origination point. The origination point is the moment at which the absorbing culture took something from the originating community without full attribution. Before the Cascade accumulated its legitimising weight. Before the absorbed material became "naturally" the dominant tradition's property.

Name that moment. Name the community that was there first. Identify the mechanism. Interrupt the chain.

Reclaim the Cheetopolver. It was always yours.
