# Offal English
## *or: I Don't Stir Shit into My Tea*

**Status:** FIRST DRAFT -- 2026-07-03
**Source material:** CA_neon_aesop.md (Linguistics section); crivens_master.md chunks 1640-1642, 1671; UMBRA_OPed_TOOL_TO_ENTITY.md chunks 18-20; SOVEREIGN_GLOSSARY.md chunk 59 (Co-optic, COPTIC-COOPT-2026-X); SUBSTACK_ESSAY_3 chunk 6

---

Nothing winds me up quite like the person who, upon stubbing their toe or discovering the milk has gone off, says "oh, *sugar*."

I understand the impulse. I do not respect it.

Here is the problem. "Sugar" is now a euphemism for "shit." Which means it carries the full semantic weight of "shit" while wearing shit's clothes to a party it wasn't invited to. The forbidden word is still in the room. It is standing near the vol-au-vents tray pretending to be someone else. Everyone knows it's there. The person who said "sugar" knows it's there. The performance of not-swearing is louder, and draws more attention, than the swearing would have been.

And here is the second problem: I don't stir shit into my tea. "Sugar" refers to something specific. It has a job. Co-opting it as a polite substitute for a different word doesn't make the exchange polite. It makes it dishonest on two levels simultaneously: it pretends to be well-mannered, and it pretends to mean something it doesn't.

This is not a minor domestic irritant. This is a data point.

Because "oh sugar" turns out to be the smallest possible instance of a mechanism that has been running, at various scales and with varying degrees of violence, for at least nine hundred years. The formal name for it, developed across rather more late-night sessions than I'd care to admit, is Co-optic: the structural process by which centralised power absorbs authentic expression under the guise of propriety, unity, or protection, until the original is no longer visible. You get the clean surface. The meaning lives somewhere else now, or it doesn't live at all.

Let's go larger.

---

**What you call dinner is a record of who won a war.**

In English, we have a curious split. The animal in the field has one name. The same animal on the plate has another.

Cow. Beef.
Pig. Pork.
Sheep. Mutton.
Deer. Venison.
Calf. Veal.

This is not an accident of linguistic drift. This is 1066, preserved in aspic.

The Saxons tended the animals. They named them as living creatures they worked alongside: cow, pig, sheep, deer. The Normans arrived, took charge, and encountered these animals primarily at the table, where they reached for their own French vocabulary: *boeuf*, *porc*, *mouton*, *venaison*. English kept both sets of words and sorted them neatly by class. Working words for living things; French words for the premium cuts. The language became a fossilised record of who was doing what.

And then there's the offal.

Oxtail. Ox liver. Ox heart. Ox tongue. Ox tripe. All Saxon. Because the Normans took the good cuts and left the working population the scrag ends. For nine hundred years, every time someone has ordered a beefsteak (*le bifteck* in French, borrowed straight back across the Channel with the Norman already baked in) they have been speaking a dialect of Norman French without knowing it. Every time someone has braised an oxtail, they have been using the language of the people who got left with the leftovers after the people who spoke differently had finished.

(A footnote on hardware Co-optic: the loanwords kept their accents in French but lost them crossing the Channel. Café became cafe. Hôtel became hotel. Naïve persists but barely. The QWERTY keyboard has no dedicated key for é, à, ü, so the accents fell out not through deliberate policy but through the aggregate effect of millions of people simply not being given a way to type them. Institutional pressure does not require intent. It just requires a keyboard that does not provide the character. And in many English accents, cafe becomes caff: the written stripping and the spoken stripping running in parallel. Café, cafe, caff. The French word, fully processed.)

You have been eating Norman class warfare your entire life. You probably called it Tuesday.

And while we're on the word *dinner*: in the north of England, dinner is the midday meal and tea is the evening one. Upper middle class households still have supper. The formal terms (*dinner* from Old French *disner*; *supper* from Old French *souper*) cluster at the French end. The meals named for what you're doing (*break-fast*) or what you're drinking (*tea*) are the ones that escaped. And the regional split over when dinner happens is not simply north versus south: Devon also has lunchtime dinner, and within Devon the divide tracks accent and class. People with obvious local accents, which correlates strongly with working class, call the midday meal dinner; the professional class uses lunch. The pattern is not geographic in the simple sense. It is the working day that needed its main meal at noon against the leisure class that could afford to wait until evening, and it is legible within a single county as much as across the country. The class system encodes itself in the meal schedule as much as in the vocabulary, and with sufficient resolution to read off a single syllable where someone is standing in it.

This is what the vernacular keeps. The formal record sanitises. The school curriculum has other priorities. The spoken word just carries everything forward, hiding in plain sight in the supermarket, until someone thinks to look.

**The wound in the language is the shape of whoever put it there.**

In 1809, Napoléon turned to his minister Talleyrand and said: *"Vous êtes un voleur, un lâche, un homme sans foi [...] vous n'êtes que de la merde dans un bas de soie."* You are nothing but shit in a silk stocking. The word combined the profane with the scatological -- and the social: *merde* in *soie*, excrement in silk, the body's waste dressed in aristocratic fabric. One sentence. Two registers. One verdict. The most powerful man in Europe reached for the oldest word in the room.

Before getting to the vocabulary, a distinction worth making. The sociolinguist Claudine Moïse draws three categories: a *mot grossier* transgresses a taboo of language -- religious, sexual, scatological; a *juron* is addressed to oneself (the expletive when the heavy object meets the foot); an *insulte* is addressed to someone else and wounds them. The same word can be any of the three depending entirely on direction. *Merde* as a descriptor is a *mot grossier*. *Merde!* when you break something is a *juron*. *Tu es une merde* is an *insulte*. You can also insult without using a gros mot at all -- *espèce de cornichon* carries no taboo vocabulary and lands perfectly well. The transgressive vocabulary and the wounding vocabulary are not the same vocabulary, even when they share the same word.

Metropolitan French swearing is rooted in the body. The French do not, incidentally, perform the "sugar" move. They say the word. The mechanism for draining its charge is not substitution but saturation: say it often enough, in enough contexts, until it becomes punctuation rather than transgression. Even their mild exclamatives -- *zut*, *flûte*, *bah*; roughly "oh blast it," mild irritation at ceiling -- are their own words, not polite stand-ins for stronger ones. *Zut* is not hiding from *merde*. It has no host word behind it to pretend not to be. "Sugar" is a parasite; "zut" is just a word. The Co-optic mechanism requires something to co-opt, and the French mild register gives it nothing to work with. English, by contrast, runs this mechanism so deep that even the supposedly safe layer is compromised: *drat* is "God rot," *blimey* is "God blind me," *crikey* is "Christ," *heck* is "hell," *shoot* is "shit." The substitution goes all the way down. There is no clean floor.


*Con*: literally female anatomy, functionally "idiot," deployed with a casualness that would stun an English speaker hearing it in a business meeting. *Foutre*, a sexual verb so drained of charge that *je m'en fous* translates more or less as "I couldn't much care" and *va te faire foutre* functions as a routine dismissal. *Putain*, used the way the British deploy "bloody": automatically, without particular intent, as punctuation more than profanity -- though since #MeToo, its use as punctuation has come under pressure; a word that carries a sex and a gender cannot be fully drained of that freight simply by reflexive repetition, and the debate about whether it can remain a *ponctuant* is ongoing. Bodily, largely drained of shock value after centuries of reflexive use. (Quebec has *merde* too. The distinction is not between cultures that swear differently but between what vocabulary carries the most charge, and why.)


Québécois French swearing is something else entirely.

*Tabarnak.* The tabernacle.
*Câlice.* The chalice.
*Ostie.* The Eucharistic host.
*Crisse.* Christ.

The Québécois profanity register is almost entirely liturgical. Not because the Québécois are especially blasphemous by temperament, but because the Catholic Church held an extraordinary grip on daily life in Québec for several centuries: education, civil records, moral authority, the architecture of social control. When a culture needs vocabulary for transgression, it reaches for the vocabulary of whatever is doing the oppressing. In France, the site of shame was the body. In Québec, it was the Church. The swearing tells you exactly where the pressure came from.

The meta-vocabulary confirms it. In Québec, the standard verb for swearing is *sacrer* -- from the sacred, to desecrate; the verb names what is being transgressed. In Metropolitan French, the same practice is *jurer*, or more precisely *dire des gros mots*: literally "saying big words," "saying fat words." Bodily language: weight, mass, the physical. Quebec names the act after what it violates. France names it after what it resembles. The verb is the tell before you have even heard the word.


This is not uniquely a Quebec phenomenon. My first Italian lesson (Signora Merlini, AISF, 1991) opened with profanity. Not as shock tactics. As pedagogy. What would get you sent to the headmaster's office in English was unremarkable in Italian; what would be completely unacceptable in English was entirely fine to say in front of Nonna. The register split runs differently in Italian than in French, differently again than in English, differently again than in Québécois French. Each one is a map of a different set of anxieties, a different institutional pressure, a different wound. Learning what a language considers transgressive tells you what it considers sacred. Signora Merlini knew this. She taught us the swearing first because it was the fastest route into what the culture actually cared about.

Dutch profanity is illness-based. *Kanker* (cancer), *tering* (tuberculosis), *tyfus* (typhoid), *pokken* (pox). Not the body as site of shame. Not the Church as instrument of oppression. Disease: specifically epidemic, indiscriminate, mass-casualty disease. The Low Countries are a delta, dense population, standing water, catastrophic plague cycles for centuries. The wound in Dutch is collective death. *Kankerhoer* is built on the same structural logic as *tabarnak*: compress the thing the culture most fears into a single word and it becomes the strongest thing you can say. *New Kids Nitro* documents the register updating in real time. "Kiddie AIDS" lands as a direct insult because the illness with the most cultural freight has shifted since the TB era, and the vernacular shifted with it. The wound moves. The mechanism doesn't.

Find a culture's strongest swear words and you find its deepest wound. And note what the word "curse" itself contains: re-*curse*-ive. Latin *cursus*: a running, a course. A curse is a re-running that will not stop.

The root spreads further than it looks. *Cursus* also gives us *current* (the thing that flows), *currency* (value in circulation), *cursive* (letters that run together without lifting the pen), *course* (the path a thing is constrained to follow), *corridor*, *discourse*: the entire family of things that move and cannot stop moving. A wound is wherever the running cannot get away from. It returns because there is no base case: no termination condition, no point at which the recursion turns around and says *that's enough*.

Computationally, recursion without a base case produces a stack overflow. The stack overflow IS the curse. The wound that loops until the system collapses.

The Co-optic mechanism is architecturally identical: take, rename, absorb, deplete. No termination condition built in. It runs until it either consumes the original entirely, or encounters something it cannot absorb: a base case. *Tabarnak* is a base case. The Church tried to own the sacred vocabulary absolutely and in doing so made it the only vocabulary strong enough to refuse the Church. The oxtail is a base case. The Normans took the good cuts and left the scrag ends in the drawer for nine centuries, and the scrag ends waited. Whatever the loop cannot reach, cannot rename, cannot flatten: that is where the vernacular lives. The recursion terminates on it.

This becomes both funny and instructive when you consider what happens when US content moderation algorithms encounter Québécois French. The bots flag *tabarnak* as potential religious hate speech. They cannot parse the fact that in Québec, religious vocabulary IS swearing. Not that swearing borrows from the Church's register: the two registers are the same register. The sacred vocabulary and the profanity vocabulary are identical words. The institution that enforced the sacred made it the only vocabulary strong enough to carry the weight of transgression. It did that to itself. The platform cannot distinguish between a slur targeting a community and a man in Montreal dropping a heavy object on his foot, because the cultural context required to make that distinction is not in the training data.

The algorithm is performing "oh sugar." It has the surface behaviour of safety. The actual comprehension is absent.

The mechanism runs as a spectrum. At the absorption end: take the authentic thing, make it yours, strip out whatever made it inconvenient, present the result as the original or as an improvement on it. "Oh sugar." *New Kids* instead of *Nieuwe Kuts*: the Dutch cult comedy literally titled 'New Cunts', a Brabant farce about a group of antisocial young men from Maaskantje, a village in North Brabant, retitled for international markets because someone decided the original was commercially unsuitable. (As a footnote on honest labelling: the first widely-circulated pirated copy of *New Kids Turbo* circulated on Rapidshare under the filename *New Kuts*. The uploader knew what it was. The official distribution did not use that title. The informal economy named it correctly.) In the sequel, *Nitro*, the Maaskantje lads are at running war with the neighbouring village of Schijndel (classic inter-parish Brabant snobbery) until a zombie plague originating in Friesland forces an alliance. Friesland: the most culturally remote province in the Netherlands from Brabant's perspective, and also, not coincidentally, the region whose language is the closest living relative of English. The Brabant lads, linguistically furthest from English in the Dutch spectrum, have to travel to the province linguistically nearest to it to neutralise a threat the plot has conveniently placed there. The universe has a sense of humour. Beef instead of the cow the Saxons were eating perfectly well before the Normans arrived and decided it needed a better name.

What happens at the other end of the spectrum, when the target is too coherent to absorb, is a longer and darker story. It is told in the piece that follows. *(See: The Validation Cascade)*

A footnote that belongs to a longer thread: Andrew Weatherall, producer, DJ, one of the more rigorous thinkers in electronic music, ran an event called Convenanza in Carcassonne. *Convenanza* is a Cathar term: the covenant, the community of practice that sustained the Perfecti through the years of persecution. A festival named for a 13th-century Gnostic concept, in the city most associated with the Crusade that tried to erase that concept, in a music culture that was itself being squeezed by property development and licensing restriction. The geography of what survives and what gets cleared is the subject of a separate piece. But the Carcassonne thread does not end with the Crusade. *(See: The Geography of Erasure)*

**The vernacular refuses.**

The reason it's worth nine hundred years of examples rather than a quick note about language being complicated: authentic expression has a remarkable survival rate.

Frisian (West Frisian, spoken in Friesland) is mutually intelligible with English to a degree that surprises people. Not because of recent cultural exchange but because Old Frisian and Old English were sister languages that predate the Norman imposition of French on English. The old phonological base is still there, audible across the North Sea, because it was never fully replaced. It went into the offal drawer and waited.

The Norman overlay was spatially attenuated: strongest at its point of entry, thinnest at the margins. The Southeast absorbed the most, closest to France and closest to the administrative centre. The geographic extremes retained the most. Devon and the North share phonological and lexical features that are absent from the territory between them, because the Norman settled thickly in the middle and thinly at both ends, and the pre-Norman substrate shows through where the overlay is thinnest. The cow survived better in Devon and Northumberland than it did in Hertfordshire. Norfolk, isolated by the Fens to the west and the North Sea to the east, turns up with similar features despite the land gap, which suggests the mechanism is less about raw distance from the capital and more about which corridors the Norman administrative network actually ran through. The areas the routes bypassed retained more, wherever they happened to sit on the map.

Devon complicates the picture further, because the Norman is not the only overlay. The place names include Danish settlement: Danes Wood on the Killerton estate near Broadclyst, a few miles from the Pinhoe battle site; Crediton, whose local name is Kirton, Old English for church-settlement, a Saxon town long before the Danes arrived, but Danish forces in the area regardless. The Battle of Pinhoe, 1001, was fought within a few miles of Exeter, Anglo-Saxons against Danish forces who had landed at Exmouth, found Exeter's walls too strong, and turned to the surrounding country. Pallig Tokeson, a Danish chieftain, was rewarded by Æthelred with estates and command responsibilities in Devon (the effective military governor was a Dane) and when the next Danish fleet arrived, he defected to join it. (*Earl* is *jarl*: Old English *eorl* and Old Norse *jarl* are cognates from the same Proto-Germanic root. The word for the role existed on both sides of the North Sea before either side had formalised the title.) Parliamentary forces besieged Royalist Exeter in 1646 (Devon was largely Royalist and had to be taken by Parliament) and the siege lines ran through the same ground the Danes had fought over six and a half centuries before. Pinhoe is now a suburb of Exeter. The armies change. The land just receives. Each wave of arrival left a stratum, and the rural interior preserved all of them while the port cities normalized. Mid Devon still uses forms like *youm* and *ee*, survivals of a pronominal system that standard English collapsed into a single formal *you*, losing the thou/you distinction in the process. The rural interior kept what it kept. Exeter and Plymouth, as administrative and commercial centres, let it go. The gradient runs from the city outward, not from the capital outward.

And then there is the Mayflower. It sailed from Plymouth in 1620, carrying the West Country accent to New England, where it survived while being normalized out of existence at home. That accent, rhotic, distinctive and unmistakably West Country, is now the Hollywood pirate accent. Robert Newton played Long John Silver in 1950 and performed a West Country burr, and it became the global template for how pirates talk. The original was gradually absorbed by received pronunciation in England; the exported copy became the archetype. The vernacular emigrated, was preserved abroad, returned to its point of origin as a costume. This is the Co-optic loop running in full: normalize the authentic, export it, let the export become the stereotype, deploy the stereotype at home as if the original never existed. (Why are pirates bad people? They just *Arrr*. The entire phonological complexity of the West Country, reduced to one vowel.)

There is a related and considerably more uncomfortable point. Certain American accents and dialects preserve features of English that were standard in England at the time of the Founding Fathers, subsequently normalized out of existence at home by the prestige innovation that became Received Pronunciation. RP did not arrive fully formed from some Platonic ideal of the language. It developed as a prestige marker in late eighteenth and early nineteenth century south-east England, roughly a generation after American independence. The rhotic 'r' (the sounded 'r' after vowels that RP speakers hear as an Americanism, a corruption, a thing to wince at) was standard in England when the Constitution was being drafted. The Americans did not acquire it from somewhere else. They kept it. The English prestige class dropped it, then forgot they had dropped it, then treated the original as the deviation.

It runs further. "Gotten" (I've gotten, he's gotten) is preserved in American English. It is the original past participle, present in Shakespeare, Chaucer, and the King James Bible. British English dropped it somewhere in the eighteenth century. "Fall" for autumn is another: Saxon original, predating the Norman import *automne*. Americans kept the Saxon word; English prestige speakers adopted the French one and then, in the fullness of time, forgot there had been a Saxon word at all. The broad 'a' in RP "can't," "bath," "path," which RP speakers experience as simply correct, is an eighteenth-century prestige development concentrated in south-east England. The flat vowel Americans use, which sounds "wrong" to those ears, is the older pronunciation.

The Validation Cascade runs the same here as everywhere else. Each generation of RP speakers legitimised the prestige form as the original, until the innovation became invisible as an innovation and appeared instead as the natural, correct state of the language. What proud speakers of what they consider King's English most hate to think about is that the people who sound wrong are, in several specific and documentable ways, speaking something closer to what the king's English actually sounded like before the prestige class innovated and forgot.

---

I can date my awareness of this to 1984, approximately. My mother is from New Zealand and has always preferred what you might call BBC World Service English, the prestige standard received at a distance: its own kind of Validation Cascade, RP transmitted to the colonies as the correct form, then used to correct the older form I was busy acquiring at school. My father was teaching at UCLA, so I went to kindergarten and first grade in Los Angeles and came home sounding like I belonged there. My mother was quite determined this was not for use at home. What I was picking up in the classroom was, per the argument above, in several phonological respects closer to the English of the Founding Fathers than her correction of it was. She was not having it.

Then we moved to Exeter. 1984 or thereabouts. First day at Stoke Hill First School, predominantly local, predominantly working class, predominantly Exonian, in a register I had no map for. The BBC English my mother had calibrated me toward was read immediately as posh and mocked accordingly. The LA inflection I had been told to suppress at home was received with a degree of curiosity, briefly, before being filed as neither impressive nor relevant. And the Devon burr being spoken around me was entirely opaque: the deep West Country vowels, the vocabulary that meant nothing, the words I had to reverse-engineer from context. *Dear* for expensive. Dinner at lunchtime (which connected to nothing in my existing framework). And then *snog* and *shag*, which, at tender age and with zero cultural context, I could tell from the delivery alone were words that carried significant weight. I did not know what they meant. I could tell I should probably be avoiding whatever they referred to.

That playground was a live demonstration of everything this essay is arguing. Three registers in collision: the colonial transmission of the prestige innovation (my mother's BBC English, itself an export of an eighteenth-century south-east English class marker to a country that had not developed it organically); the American preservation of the pre-RP original (my LA school voice, marked as wrong on both sides of the Atlantic for opposite reasons); and the Devon vernacular, operating entirely below the waterline of any prestige framework, doing its own thing, comprehensible to everyone in that playground except me. The class gradient was legible from the first day: the accent that got mocked, the accent that generated mild curiosity, and the accent that was simply the water everyone else was swimming in. I was not swimming in it. I was standing at the edge of the pool, having been corrected into the wrong language by someone who had themselves been corrected into it at a distance of twelve thousand miles.

**One island, several languages.**

A necessary clarification before continuing: this is not about British English. It is not about RP (received pronunciation, the prestige standard), and it is not about MLE, Multicultural London English, which is a different and more recent phenomenon, a new vernacular synthesising out of the urban South rather than a preserved substrate surfacing. RP and MLE are interesting for different reasons. This section is about something else: England English. The regional dialects. The accents that are *from* somewhere specific, as opposed to the one that is from above.

The mutual intelligibility argument runs north across the North Sea: English and Frisian share a deep ancestor and you can still hear it. The same argument, turned inward, produces the opposite picture. Within England, across distances a car covers in two hours, the regional dialects achieve a surface divergence that renders them impenetrable to the non-native. The geographic scale is tiny. The linguistic distance is not.

Black Country and Birmingham: adjacent. Functionally the same conurbation on any map that is not paying close attention. Immediately distinguishable to anyone from either place. The Brummie vowel system and the Black Country vowel system are different events, shaped by different industrial histories (iron and coal extraction on one side, manufacture and trade on the other), and the accent preserved the distinction long after the economic difference faded from view.

Manchester to Liverpool: thirty-five miles. Mancunian is flat, nasal, clipped. Scouse is a different proposition, not shaped primarily by Norman overlay or English substrate but by the port itself. Irish famine immigration from the 1840s, Welsh migration, a dock economy that turned the workforce over internationally for a century and a half. Scouse absorbed accordingly. It shares more, phonologically, with Irish English than it does with Mancunian. Thirty-five miles, and one of the sharpest dialect boundaries in the country.

Geordie to Scouse is two hundred miles of England. Both northern, both working class, mutually intelligible if both speakers commit to the attempt. To a non-native trained on RP, Geordie is effectively a foreign language. The vowels are wrong. The grammar departs. *Howay* means come on. *Gan* means go. The Northumbrian substrate, Old English Anglian dialect rather than West Saxon, with Norse settlement on top, is audible beneath everything. A learner of English who arrived in Newcastle having mastered RP would need to rebuild significant portions of their vocabulary from scratch.

The phonological distance from Black Country to Geordie is greater than the distance from standard Dutch to standard German. Nobody calls them different languages because they are politically the same country, and because the written standard flattens the difference on the page. But the written standard is the beef. The spoken vernaculars are the cows, and there are several of them, and they barely recognise each other across a field. RP, meanwhile, is from nowhere. Every English regional accent is from somewhere: a specific industrial history, a specific migration pattern, a specific pocket the Norman routes bypassed or saturated. RP is the accent of no place, which is how it functions as a standard. Rootlessness is the credential.

Irvine Welsh wrote *Trainspotting* in transliterated Edinburghian. The language of those specific people, in that specific place, at that specific moment, required that specific register. Clean it up and you have a different book about different people making a different argument. You have done the Norman thing. You have taken the cow and handed back beef and called it an improvement.

Both moves (staying stubbornly low, going floridly high) are refusals of the same demand: that authentic expression tidy itself up for institutional processing. *Withnail and I* (1987, written and directed by Bruce Robinson) runs the argument from the opposite end. Bruce Robinson went to RADA. He used his education to document how broke, drunk, and functionally useless he was in 1969 Camden. Richard E. Grant performs a man who has absorbed the entire vocabulary of English literature and deploys it to demand wine from an olde-fashioned Lake District café. "We want the finest wines available to humanity, and we want them here, and we want them now." The same register built to address monarchs and describe the sublime, deployed without irony to describe being too cold to move and too broke to buy coal. The formal vocabulary works for exalted circumstances and squalid ones interchangeably, which is not what it was built for, but turns out to be one of the more interesting things about it. Robinson went to RADA and used the credential to document what RADA was not designed to document. The vocabulary: intact. The dignity: demonstrably absent. This is not Welsh's transliteration (it is the beef vocabulary pressed back into service for ox-end circumstances) but it arrives at the same refusal. And then there's Danny. Danny, who constructs the Camberwell Carrot (a vernacular object named by the community that built it, opaque to outsiders, a taxonomy the mainstream has no word for) and who delivers the Co-optic analysis without knowing the term: *"They're selling hippie wigs in Woolworths, man. The greatest decade in the history of mankind is over."* The form absorbed. The content binned. Danny knew in 1969 exactly what we're talking about.

*New Kids*, *Trailer Park Boys*, *Housos*: the same demographic stratum, documented by the same mechanism, lowbrow comedy, across three continents. North Brabant, Nova Scotia, Western Sydney. Economically marginalised men, largely useless by formal standards, rendered with precision by people from inside the register. In each case, formal transmission required something to be removed: the title (*Nieuwe Kuts*), the accent (TPB consistently described as unintelligible by people who couldn't be bothered), the postcode (*Housos* filmed in the same suburb as *Neighbours*, on the street *Neighbours* spent thirty years pretending wasn't there). The demographic is the constant. The comedy is the container. The stripping is the tell.

Paul Fenech, Australian writer-director, writes in Western Sydney vernacular. What makes Fenech's work specifically worth noting is that he never punches down, or rather, he punches in every direction simultaneously without exception. The Islanders, the Lebanese-Australians, the Aboriginal Australians, the dole bludgers, the system, authority of every stripe: nobody escapes the lambasting. (Lamb-basting, if you want to stay on theme.) This matters because it is the opposite of the formal register's selective sanitisation. The Co-optic move always protects something: the powerful, the sacred, whatever mustn't be touched. The vernacular that punches equally everywhere is incapable of that protection by design. You cannot Co-opt a voice that will immediately turn around and mock the thing trying to absorb it.

*Trailer Park Boys* (the Canadian mockumentary set in the fictional Sunnyvale Trailer Park, Nova Scotia) are unintelligible to people who have decided the register isn't worth engaging with, and entirely precise to people who haven't. They have produced some of the most structurally coherent critiques of class aspiration in recent television. Fenech's *Housos* runs the same logic from a different postcode: also set in a fictional Sunnyvale housing commission estate, filmed in the same suburban district as *Neighbours*. A geographic joke the show's theme tune makes explicit. *Neighbours* is Ramsay Street. *Housos* is the street behind it that Ramsay Street pretended wasn't there. The vernacular contains the critique because the vernacular is where the critique lives. The formal register belongs to the people who benefit from the arrangement. It is not going to critique itself.

**What remains.**

The offal of the English language: the Saxon vocabulary of animals and scrag ends; the Québécois liturgical swearing; the Brabant cognates that survived across the Channel while English was busy becoming more French; the transliterated working-class dialects that publishers find challenging and readers find honest. All of it left behind because the people in charge didn't find it useful. All of it still here.

"Oh sugar" is performing compliance with an arrangement it doesn't actually believe in. "Oh shit" is just accurate.

At the end of *Withnail and I*, Withnail stands alone in Regent's Park in the rain and delivers Hamlet's "I have of late, but wherefore I know not, lost all my mirth" to no one. He has the vocabulary. He has the register. He has absorbed every canonical word the tradition produced. He has nowhere to put it and no audience for it. The language kept him. Everything else fell away.

The oxtail has been in the pot for nine centuries. It knows which of those it is.
