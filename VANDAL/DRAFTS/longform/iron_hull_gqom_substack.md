# The Iron Hull

### *What a Durban township, a Brussels warehouse and a 1973 Herbie Hancock record have in common*

---

There is a club somewhere in Durban called 101.

Its owner, Ashoo Rampersad, explained the problem to Mixmag a few years back. The music his customers made, he said, "has certain associations and brings with it certain behaviors." Other venues would not book it. It comes from poor townships, he was told. It is played on public transport used by the poor. It is associated with drugs and violence. It is not associated with wealth or glamour.

The music was gqom. Pronounced *qwom*, with a Zulu tongue click at the start and a throaty "om" that closes like a kick drum hitting. The name itself mimics the sound.

The venues that turned it down have since booked it. The DJs who made it in their bedrooms in Clermont and Umlazi and Inanda are now on festival lineups in London and Brussels. Beyoncé sampled one of the tracks. The music did not change to deserve this. The world caught up.

This is a piece about how that happens, and what it costs, and how it always costs the same people.

---

## Durban is Not Where You Think

Gqom is a sub-genre of house music. It emerged in the early 2010s from the townships of Durban, KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa, and it sounds like nothing else on earth. The kick drum does not fall where you expect it to. The hi-hat patterns cut across the bar in ways that create a constant low-level anxiety, a sense that the music is about to resolve into something familiar and is perpetually refusing to. There are no chord progressions in the conventional sense. No melody you could hum. Just percussion, bass, and the specific, forensic application of rhythmic pressure.

Kode9, the Hyperdub boss, described listening to it as "being suspended over the gravitational field of a black hole." Simon Reynolds, in his writing on somatic music, placed it in a lineage that runs from industrial through EBM through techno, a tradition where the music's primary function is not emotional communication but physical induction: you are not supposed to feel something; you are supposed to be physically altered by it. He was writing about European and American music. Gqom got there independently.

Durban is the Miami of South Africa, which is not a comparison either city would immediately welcome but is structurally accurate. A beach town. Similar climate to South Florida. A North Beach and a South Beach strip, both lined with parties that run into the next morning. All-night party culture. Taxi drivers given the latest tracks as a form of promotion. Track titles borrowed from pill culture: Mitsubishi, Mercedes, and the specific grammar of a particular kind of desire.

From inside this specific environment, in Durban's northern townships, very young men with cheap hardware and cracked copies of FruityLoops built something the world did not yet have a name for.

## The Practitioners

**DJ Lag**, born in Clermont, was part of what he called the Rainbow generation: the first cohort of South Africans born after apartheid ended. He describes the origins straightforwardly. "Guys like Culoe De Song, Nakedboys and Blaq Soul were doing a really great job mixing more traditional music with house. I just wanted to join the type of genre that they were doing but we ended up doing something else. It was broken beats. Nobody knew what we were doing but it eventually became gqom."

He was among the first to put a music video to it. *Ice Drop*. The video gave people outside Durban their first look at what the culture was: the backyard parties, the bhenga dancing (all wavy arms, toe taps and wobbly knees), the vernacular architecture of the township neighbourhood as a site of sonic production. The world saw it and wanted to know more.

A few years later, Beyoncé incorporated his track *Trip to New York* into her Global Citizen performance. That led to *My Power*. That led to something DJ Lag noticed: "A lot of big artists who are doing really good in South Africa, like Cassper Nyovest, Babes Wodumo and Okmalumkoolkat, started rapping and doing kwaito on gqom beats. That's where it got more popular, because all that music was being played on radio a lot."

The international attention did not just bring bookings. It gave the music permission to exist in its own country.

Alongside Lag: **Dankie Boi**, **DJ Nazi**, **Blacks Jr** in the underground production layer. **Dladla Mshunqisi** and **Phakamani Samukelo** as vocalists who gave the form a human face without softening its structure. **DJ Tira**, whose infrastructure at Afrotainment routed gqom toward a mainstream South African audience without making it tidy. **||ALA|MEDA||** in a later, more internationally visible iteration. **Sho Madjozi**, working across Tsonga, Swahili, English and the various registers those languages carry with them, demonstrating what outward translation looks like when you do not capitulate.

## The Body Goes Viral

The bhenga was always the distribution mechanism. Before streaming, before TikTok, before anything, the dance was how gqom moved. YouTube videos of amateur dancers in backyard parties. Wavy arms, toe taps, wobbly knees. The *Guardian* noted it in 2016: people drawing crowds by dancing in circles, each performance an advertisement for the sound that made it possible. Gqom was spread body-first. You heard it because you saw someone move to it.

TikTok changed the scale. It did not change the logic.

It is worth pausing on what the bhenga actually is, because it matters for understanding why it travels the way it does. The bhenga is not freestyle. African dance traditions are typically collective and choreographed: moves are known, shared, transmitted, performed together in a common physical vocabulary. The synchronisation is the point. It is the grammar of community expressed through the body, a language that requires others to speak it properly.

This makes it the structural opposite of what happened to European club culture when acid house arrived in the late 1980s. One of acid house's defining cultural effects was the liberation of the dancefloor from formalised, synchronised movement. Before acid house, you needed to know the moves: the northern soul vocabulary, the disco steps, the dancehall choreography. The dancefloor had a literacy requirement. After acid house, the point was precisely not to know the moves, to dissolve individual self-consciousness into collective formlessness, to give the average shy person permission to exist in their body without demonstrating technical competence. The eyes-closed, self-contained rave shuffle was a democratisation of physical expression, an opening of the dancefloor to everyone who had previously been too inhibited to enter it.

Gqom and the bhenga arrive at the same collective dancefloor experience through the exact opposite mechanism. The synchronisation is not a barrier; it is the invitation. You learn the moves because knowing the moves means belonging. And on TikTok, the shared vocabulary travels intact: the dance challenge format is, structurally, the digital transmission of a physical language from one body to another, across thousands of miles, at no cost to anyone except the producers whose music is making it possible.

What nobody planned, and what the numbers confirm, is that the bhenga also happens to be structurally optimised for the algorithm. TikTok's recommendation engine rewards completion rate and replay: content that makes people watch to the end, then watch again. Synchronised movement creates visual rhythm. Visual rhythm creates compulsion. High-contrast choreography, bodies moving in unison and then breaking into individual expression and then snapping back into formation, drives the eye in ways that formless, individual dancing does not. The challenge format generates duets and response videos, which the algorithm reads as high-engagement signal and amplifies accordingly. The result is a feedback loop: the platform rewards the content, the content reaches more people, more people attempt the challenge, the challenge generates more content, the algorithm rewards it again. 

The bhenga hacked TikTok without knowing what TikTok was.

Tracks like *Mopepe* generated millions of views through the dance challenge format, the moves doing the promotional work the Durban taxi drivers used to do, except globally and at effectively zero cost. The sound of a specific neighbourhood in KwaZulu-Natal, made on cheap hardware by people in their early twenties, appeared on screens in Seoul and São Paulo and Stoke-on-Trent in the same week. The algorithm optimised for the dance. The dance carried the music. The music found an audience its makers could not have predicted and would not have been able to reach through any conventional industry route.

Consider the TikTok account @lulthop, run by a creator who goes by Peanuts, whose entire channel is nothing but dance clips to gqom, kwaito and amapiano tracks. No commentary. No production. Just the body, the beat, and the camera. Each video is an unofficial music video. Each view is a unit of promotion. The account documents three genres simultaneously, functions as an organic archive of their dance cultures, and drives streams to tracks whose producers were not consulted and will not be paid a proportionate share of the value being created. Peanuts himself, notably, presents as considerably more affluent than most other creators performing the same challenges across the platform, whether from family background, TikTok creator payments, or both. If the platform is paying anyone in this chain, it is the distributor.

The videos routinely reach millions of views. Some reach tens of millions. The visual register is often aspirational: the choreography confident, the energy abundant, the moves carrying the implicit promise of a different life that the music has always been pointing toward. Gqom has always been aspirational, even at its darkest. The pill names in the track titles (Mitsubishi, Mercedes, Rolex, Ferrari, and the rest) were drug references and aspirational markers simultaneously, which is not a contradiction. European rave culture stamped its MDMA tablets with brand logos; the stamp functioned as a mark of quality, and the quality mark borrowed its authority from the brand's aspirational value. The two things were the same thing. The choices were specific and literate: Mitsubishi meant the Lancer Evolution, the WRC rally car, not the Colt. Rolex, Mercedes, Ferrari: performance and luxury, not generic comfort. South Africans who made it to European club scenes encountered that culture directly, and some were involved in its distribution networks. The names came back as genuine cultural artifacts of real contact, not imagined elsewhere. For those who hadn't made the trip, the same names functioned as aspirational shorthand: a taxonomy of a world the music was pointing toward. Both things were true at once. They usually are.

And then you notice the uniforms. @lulthop is primarily Peanuts himself, performing with what appear to be family members and classmates from the same school. Whether it is a private school is hard to determine with certainty, but it does not look bad. The setting is comfortable, the energy relaxed, the choreography polished in the way that comes from time and space to practise. But @lulthop is one channel among hundreds participating in the same phenomenon, and across those hundreds the picture is considerably wider. Work uniforms from across the South African economic spectrum: entry-level retail staff, security guards, people who appear to be in professional roles several rungs above that. South Africa has a more entrenched workplace uniform culture than most Western countries. In the UK, a work uniform typically means a supermarket or a logistics depot; offices are business casual, building sites generic PPE. In South Africa, employers across a much wider professional range insist on specific dress, and what reads as a uniform can indicate almost any level of the working economy. And then people in actual dance studios, which implies training, resources, leisure time, an aspiration at least partially realised.

And then the channels that are obviously not in South Africa at all. Some are clearly filmed in the UK. Some in France. The same moves, the same music, the same challenge format, performed by people who did not grow up with this tradition, in cities a long way from Durban. The bhenga has left the building.

The effect of this range is to make visible something the music has always understood about itself: gqom does not belong to one economic position within the community that made it. It cuts across the full width of a working life in post-apartheid South Africa, from the child still inside the educational system to the professional who has arrived somewhere and is still dancing toward somewhere else. The aspiration encoded in the music is not simply about class mobility. It is about a kind of freedom that exists on the other side of whatever system you are currently inside, and every uniform, regardless of what it represents, is a system.

The image contains the whole argument, though the argument is less clean than it first appears. TikTok cites the track name and producer on screen. Royalties flow: not nothing, but whatever the platform decides to pay, at rates it sets and periodically restructures without consultation. A producer whose track genuinely goes viral will receive something. South Africa's weak rand means that something in dollar terms can travel considerably further in Durban than the same number would in London or Paris, which introduces a layer of ambiguity the simpler version of this story prefers to ignore. The dancers receive nothing regardless, which is a separate and more straightforward injustice. What the platform captures is not the money, or not only the money. It captures the terms. It sets the rate. It owns the audience relationship. It can restructure the royalty network at will, and the producers, wherever they are, are price-takers. They have global reach they could never have bought through conventional channels and negotiating leverage they never acquired in exchange for it.

The other side of this, which the simpler version of the story tends to elide: being in the right place when a trend breaks can matter enormously, and this is a structural possibility that did not exist at scale before these platforms. A producer in Durban whose track goes genuinely viral at the moment gqom crosses over is not in the same position as a session musician from the wrong decade in the wrong city. Platform micropayments that look trivial from London (fractions of a penny per stream, royalty statements that would not cover a monthly Oyster card) can represent something structurally different in an economy where every dollar goes further. The infrastructure of global distribution may be, in specific circumstances, more empowering at the periphery than at the centre. This is not a defence of the terms. It is a complication of the narrative, and any argument about extraction that ignores it is doing its own kind of simplification.

## The Connection Nobody Expected

In the 1980s, in Europe, something happened in music that was not supposed to be music. Industrial first: Throbbing Gristle in London, Einstürzende Neubauten in Berlin. The Neubauten name translates directly: Collapsing New Buildings. They made music from demolition equipment, from the sounds of structures coming down, in a continent still clearing its postwar rubble. One culture grows as another declines; the falling building and the art it generates are part of the same event. From industrial, in Belgium, came Electronic Body Music: Front 242. Neon Judgement. Klinik. Post-punk machine music made by white, urban, European musicians with very specific theoretical frameworks about technology and the body and the machine.

On the surface, gqom and EBM have nothing in common.

Structurally, they are the same argument.

Both deploy percussion as the primary carrier of meaning, not melody, not harmony, percussion. Both reduce their sonic vocabulary to the minimum necessary to generate the desired physical effect. Both refuse harmonic resolution. Both sound, in the most productive sense of the word, *hostile*. Both are, in the technical phrase Simon Reynolds uses, Body Music: organised around the production of specific physical states in a collective body, rather than around communication with an individual listener.

Two communities in radically different historical circumstances pushed in the same direction and arrived at the same place. This is not coincidence. It is what happens when you strip away the conventions of Western pop music and follow the logic of physical impact all the way to its conclusion. The human body is not a cultural variable. Bass frequencies and rhythmic displacement work the same way everywhere.

## Where It Started

To understand gqom, you need to understand kwaito.

Kwaito emerged in South Africa in the early 1990s, in the specific historical moment of apartheid's ending. It was not simply a new music; it was a music that was impossible before that moment. Slow, heavy, repetitive. Emphasis on sub-bass and deep kick drum. Vocal chanting rather than melodic singing. Deliberately minimal in ways that Western critics often misread as poverty of imagination.

There is an argument, made by South African cultural scholars, that kwaito's minimalism encodes a social philosophy: that the music's function is to create a shared sonic space for collective self-determination. A space that belongs to the community that made it, governed by rules that community sets, inaccessible to the critical frameworks of an outside observer who does not share the community's specific historical experience. Kwaito's radical repetition is not laziness. It is the sonic equivalent of the political right to be unintelligible to those who have historically claimed the right to interpret your experience.

Gqom descended from kwaito and departed from it. Faster, typically 125-140 BPM. Darker. Stripped of even the minimal melodic vocabulary kwaito retained. Associated with Durban rather than Johannesburg, a port city rather than an industrial one, with a demographic complexity (the largest concentration of people of Indian descent outside India, a large Zulu majority, a significant Coloured community) that has always made its cultural production specifically, stubbornly local.

The kick patterns are irregular in ways that make it impossible to coast. You cannot let gqom carry you. You have to navigate it.

## The Extraction Problem

Gqom producers built a global genre on hardware that costs a fraction of what a professional studio in London or New York would consider adequate. The creative intelligence and the material poverty are structurally connected. The poverty is the constraint that drives the creativity. The creativity generates cultural value that flows, through the mechanics of the global music industry, primarily to entities that did not produce it.

This is not new. Herbie Hancock's Mwandishi period, the cosmic jazz band that laid some of this groundwork in the early 1970s, was commercially abandoned by Columbia Records. The founding fathers of Detroit techno, Atkins, May, Saunderson, built an international genre from bedroom studios in a city the American economy had decided to abandon. The lineage is consistent: make something extraordinary with nothing, watch the value flow elsewhere.

The label infrastructure that formed around gqom was, at least initially, better-intentioned than most. **Gqom Oh!**, founded in Rome by Italian DJ Francesco "Nan Kolè" Cucchi and South African collaborator Lerato Phiri, was explicitly conceived as corrective: "My main aim is to really help these artists who don't have proper speakers or equipment to properly express their creativity," Cucchi told *The Guardian* in 2016. *Gqom Oh! The Sound of Durban Vol. 1*, released that January, was among the first compilations to make the scene legible to a European bass-music audience. London's Goon Club Allstars, introduced to the sound by Hyperdub artist Okzharp, signed DJ Lag and Rudeboyz. Kode9, Mumdance and Addison Groove began playing gqom in European clubs. **DESCENDANTS Records**, operating within South Africa, represents the domestic layer: building the commercial base from the origin point rather than the export route.

The pattern is the same regardless of intention. The creative community generates the value. The distributional infrastructure captures the majority of it.

The iron hull that gqom has built from the broken machines of industrial poverty is one of the most culturally significant sonic objects of the twenty-first century. The people who built it have been heard. They are still waiting to be paid correctly.

## The Lineage

Herbie Hancock released two albums in 1973. One of them you know. One of them made the one you know possible, and nobody talks about it.

The one you know is *Head Hunters*. Chameleon. The clavinet groove, the funk crossover, the biggest-selling jazz album of its era. Columbia Records promoted it heavily. It deserved the promotion. It is a masterpiece.

The other one is *Sextant*. No chord progressions you could hum along to. No obvious radio format. Instead: Patrick Gleeson's ARP 2600 synthesizer generating an automated, repeating electronic pulse that operates independently of the human musicians, Hancock and his band improvising around a clock they did not set, the music suspended between jazz and something that had not been named yet. Columbia let it sink. It sold a fraction of what *Head Hunters* sold. The industry had made its decision: here is the accessible thing, here is the generative thing, and we are selling the accessible thing.

This is not a complaint about *Head Hunters*. It is an observation about how the industry has always been better at selling what it already understands than at recognising what is going to matter.

*Sextant* is the criminally overlooked touchstone of the Afrofuturist electronic lineage. Juan Atkins has spoken about the influence of cosmic jazz, of Parliament/Funkadelic's outer-space mythology, of the idea that Black futurism was as legitimate a framework for electronic music as European avant-garde. Derrick May's elevator formulation positions Kraftwerk at one pole of the tension. The other pole, Clinton, the cosmic funk tradition, runs directly through the Mwandishi era, through *Sextant*, through the specific texture of *Rain Dance*'s ARP loop, which sounds like something Cybotron might have built around if they had had access to it.

*Sextant* sits in the jazz section of the record shop. The music it made possible sits in electronic. Nobody connected them officially. The lineage did not require an official connection.

The blueprint exists. The *Rain Dance* loop, time-stretched to 130 BPM, with a gqom kick matrix built around it, off-beat, asymmetric, falling where Gleeson's internal logic suggests rather than where convention demands: the template is precise and the result, on paper, sounds like it was always going to happen. The platform to realise it is still in development. This piece is, among other things, part of the argument for why it should exist.

The lineage from cosmic jazz to Detroit techno to the townships of Durban is not a chain of influence. It is a network of parallel discoveries of the same fundamental sonic philosophy, each conditioned by its specific social and technological context. The machine sets the clock. The human negotiates with it. The negotiation is the music.

Derrick May said Detroit techno sounds like George Clinton and Kraftwerk stuck in an elevator with only a sequencer to keep them company. He was describing Detroit. He was also describing Durban.

He just did not know it yet.
